How feature attribution works:

Logistic Regression predicts class sentiment using a weighted sum: z = Σ (w_i * tfidf_i) + b. The color intensity represents the product w_i * tfidf_i (word contribution). Green signals words moving the classification towards Positive, and Red signals words pulling it towards Negative.

Standard NLP text classifiers (like TF-IDF Bag-of-Words with Linear models) assume words contribute to sentiment independently. In practice, real-world customer reviews violate this assumption in predictable ways, making the model unreliable. Here are the 4 primary unreliability vectors in product reviews:

1. Negations & Valence Modifiers

Standard bag-of-words ignores word order. Phrases like "not bad" or "no issues" contain negative tokens ("bad", "issues") which the model interprets literally, leading to false negatives.

"The refurbished Fire HD arrived in perfect condition... and no issues whatsoever. not disappointed."
Actual: Positive | Predicted: Negative
2. Sarcasm & Irony

Sarcastic reviews use highly positive words ("perfect", "genius", "better off") in ironical ways. Since linear classifiers only sum word values, they predict positive confidence.

"...the stand on the back is so wonky, you are better off just using a jar of peanut butter and a couple pieces of chewing gum..."
Actual: Negative | Predicted: Positive
3. Clause Hierarchies & Logistics

Customers often praise the product but slam the delivery, app, or price. If the negative portion uses stronger words, the model misclassifies the entire review, ignoring product satisfaction.

"I like the product itself but its app is very slow esp. outside the U.S..."
Actual: Positive (4.0) | Predicted: Negative
4. Text-Rating Discrepancies

Users sometimes make mistakes, typing a positive text but accidentally clicking 1 or 2 stars, or vice-versa. The model predicts correctly based on text, but is labeled as an "error".

"Good"
Actual: Negative (2.0) | Predicted: Positive
